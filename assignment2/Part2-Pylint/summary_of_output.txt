Stylistic warnings generally encompass the convention errors. While 
these might affect readability, they aren't true issues with the code.
Some warnings under refactor, such as duplicate-code or too-many-positional-arguments,
may be indicitive of hard to maintain code. These issues may work for now but are a sign
of copy pasting or other poorly enforced standards. 


Some 'warnings' are in a similar boat. Unused parameters, unused imports, 
and similar are not essential to maintainable code, but are worth looking at.
Others, such as arguments-differ and unreachable, are more likely to be a problem.
If code is unreachable, or arguments are not enforced (as python does not), it may 
be difficult long term to understand the code. 

Warnings such as error unsubscriptable-object or not-an-iterable, are almost always
going to cause problems. Many errors are import-errors, which may be easier to catch
and may not be as much of an issue.
